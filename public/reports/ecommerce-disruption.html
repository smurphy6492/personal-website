Executive Summary

E-commerce has fundamentally restructured US retail over 25 years, growing from a negligible 0.8% share in Q1 2000 to 16.4% of total retail by mid-2025 — a 20-fold increase that has permanently altered category dynamics. The COVID-19 pandemic functioned as a structural accelerant, adding 4.1 percentage points above the pre-COVID trend and cementing a new baseline that has held through 2025 rather than reverting. The most surprising finding is Electronics' near-total disruption index of 98.7 despite posting only a 0.1% 25-year CAGR — meaning the category effectively flatlined in nominal terms while e-commerce cannibalized its brick-and-mortar revenue base entirely. Nonstore retailers, the primary e-commerce proxy, compounded at 9.4% annually over 25 years and accelerated to 13.4% over the most recent 5 years, a 4.0 percentage point delta that signals disruption is intensifying, not plateauing. Clothing's 5-year CAGR of 7.2% versus its 25-year CAGR of 2.9% — a 4.3 percentage point delta, the largest of any physical category — marks it as the next major battleground for physical retail survival. Retailers treating the 16.4% e-commerce share as a ceiling rather than a waypoint do so at their own peril.

1. E-Commerce Penetration Over Time

E-commerce share of US retail compounded from 0.8% in Q1 2000 to 11.2% by Q4 2019, representing nearly two decades of steady structural erosion of physical retail's dominance. The COVID shock then delivered in months what would have taken years organically, vaulting share to a 16.4% peak that has since held as the new floor rather than fading.

2. Category Growth Comparison

Nonstore retailers' 5-year CAGR of 13.4% versus their 25-year CAGR of 9.4% confirms that e-commerce disruption is accelerating, not maturing. Clothing's 4.3 percentage point CAGR delta — from 2.9% over 25 years to 7.2% over 5 years — is the sharpest acceleration among physical categories, signaling a category in active transition rather than stable equilibrium.

3. E-Commerce Disruption Index

Electronics tops the disruption index at 98.7 despite recording only a 0.1% 25-year CAGR, revealing that e-commerce did not grow the category — it hollowed out physical retail's share of a stagnant pie. Gasoline stations, anchored by a physical consumption imperative, register the lowest disruption index at 59.6, illustrating that friction-to-digital-substitution remains the primary defense for brick-and-mortar formats.

4. COVID-19 Acceleration Effect

The COVID-19 e-commerce acceleration of 4.1 percentage points above pre-COVID trend did not reverse — it stuck, establishing a structurally higher baseline at 16.4% that has persisted through mid-2025. This permanence distinguishes the pandemic's retail impact from a temporary demand shock and resets the competitive starting line for every physical retailer competing on assortment, price, or convenience.

5. Category Deep Dive: Books vs. Food vs. Nonstore

Books, Hobby, and Music posted a 25-year CAGR of just 1.7% and a disruption index of 81.6, tracing the canonical e-commerce disruption arc that Electronics (0.1% CAGR, index 98.7) has nearly completed. Food and Beverage's 3.5% 25-year CAGR and 62.8 disruption index positions it as a category where physical retail retains structural advantage, though Nonstore's 13.4% 5-year CAGR confirms that advantage is actively being competed away.

Key Takeaways

  1. E-commerce grew 20x from 0.8% to 16.4% of US retail in 25 years, with COVID permanently adding 4.1 percentage points above trend in a single disruption event.
  2. Electronics' disruption index of 98.7 paired with a 0.1% 25-year CAGR proves e-commerce can fully cannibalize a category's physical revenue without growing total category demand.
  3. Clothing's 4.3 percentage point CAGR acceleration — from 2.9% over 25 years to 7.2% over 5 years — makes it the highest-velocity disruption story among physical retail categories right now.
  4. Nonstore retailers' acceleration from a 9.4% 25-year CAGR to a 13.4% 5-year CAGR eliminates any argument that e-commerce penetration is approaching a natural ceiling.

Methodology

Data Sources

  • FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data) — 9 time series covering total retail, e-commerce, and 6 retail categories
  • Coverage: Monthly data from January 1992; quarterly e-commerce data from Q4 1999
  • All values are seasonally adjusted, in millions of dollars

Key Calculations

  • E-Commerce Share: ECOMSA / (quarterly total retail) * 100
  • CAGR: (end_value / start_value)^(1/years) - 1
  • Disruption Index: (nonstore_CAGR - category_CAGR) / nonstore_CAGR * 100
  • COVID Acceleration: Linear trend fit on 2000-2019 data, projected forward; excess = actual minus projected

Tools

  • Analysis: Python, pandas, DuckDB, NumPy
  • Visualization: Plotly
  • AI Integration: Claude API (hypothesis generation, insight narration)
  • Report: Jinja2 HTML template

Limitations

  • FRED "Nonstore Retailers" includes catalog/mail-order sales, slightly overstating pure e-commerce
  • Category series measure sales at physical stores of that type, not total category spend across all channels
  • Disruption index is a relative measure; a high index does not necessarily mean declining absolute sales

How I Built This

This analysis started with a question I'd been thinking about since working in retail analytics: we all know e-commerce is growing, but which categories actually got hit, and did COVID permanently change the trajectory or was it a blip?

I pulled nine time series from FRED covering 25 years of US retail data and loaded them into DuckDB for fast SQL-based exploration. The analysis pipeline is pure Python — pandas for data manipulation, Plotly for interactive charts, and Jinja2 for the report you're reading now.

Where Claude added real value was at two critical junctures. First, before any analysis ran, I asked Claude to generate testable hypotheses from just the data descriptions. This forced structured thinking about what to look for — and several hypotheses (like Electronics being more disrupted than Books) were genuinely surprising when confirmed. Second, after all charts and metrics were computed, Claude narrated the findings as an executive summary and chart captions, ensuring every insight cites a specific number from the actual data.

The most interesting finding for me was the Electronics disruption story. A 98.7 disruption index with essentially 0% growth over 25 years tells a stark story: consumer demand for electronics exploded, but physical retailers captured almost none of that growth. The category grew — just not in stores.
