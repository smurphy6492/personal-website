Executive Summary

E-commerce has grown from 0.8% of US retail in Q1 2000 to 16.4% in mid-2025, a 20x expansion that permanently restructured where and how Americans spend. COVID-19 was the single largest structural accelerator on record, adding 4.1 percentage points above the pre-COVID trend in a matter of quarters — a shift that has fully held, with the current 16.4% share standing well above the 11.2% pre-COVID baseline. The most surprising finding is the Electronics category: despite facing the most intense e-commerce disruption of any segment (disruption index 100.0), brick-and-mortar electronics retail has posted a near-zero 25-year CAGR of just 0.1%, confirming that online competitors did not merely slow the category — they absorbed virtually all its growth for a quarter century. Meanwhile, Clothing's 5-year CAGR of 7.2% against a 25-year CAGR of 2.9% signals that COVID unlocked latent digital demand in apparel that traditional store formats had suppressed. With nonstore retailers now compounding at 13.4% annually over the past five years versus 9.4% over 25 years, the acceleration is intensifying rather than plateauing, and retail strategies built on physical footprint dominance face a structurally shrinking share of total spend.

1. E-Commerce Penetration Over Time

E-commerce's share of US retail climbed from 0.8% in Q1 2000 to 11.2% by Q4 2019, then surged to a peak of 16.4% during the COVID era — a jump that took 19 years to build and less than 2 years to accelerate by 5 additional points. The 16.4% share recorded in July 2025 confirms the COVID-era gains are permanent, not a pandemic-era anomaly.

2. Category Growth Comparison

Every retail category tracked is now growing faster over the past 5 years than over the prior 25 years, but the magnitude of acceleration differs sharply: Clothing's 5-year CAGR of 7.2% is 4.3 percentage points above its 25-year CAGR of 2.9%, while Food & Beverage shows near-zero acceleration at just +0.2 percentage points. Online Retail's 5-year CAGR of 13.4% versus its 25-year CAGR of 9.4% confirms that e-commerce itself is accelerating even as it matures.

Important context: Each category measures sales at physical stores of that type, not total consumer spending in that category. When someone buys a laptop on Amazon, that sale counts under Online Retail, not Electronics. Electronics stores posting a 0.1% 25-year CAGR doesn't mean consumers stopped buying electronics. It means the explosive growth in consumer electronics spending went almost entirely to online channels. The physical store pie didn't shrink dramatically; it just didn't grow while the total market ballooned around it.

3. E-Commerce Disruption Index

Electronics tops the disruption index at 100.0, reflecting a 25-year CAGR of just 0.1% — meaning the entire category's growth over a quarter century was effectively captured by online channels. Furniture (57.3) and Books/Hobby/Music (56.8) rank second and third, while Gasoline Stations score 0.9, confirming that physical-necessity categories anchored by fuel remain structurally insulated from digital displacement.

The permanent COVID acceleration of +4.1 percentage points above the pre-COVID trend is the defining structural break in 25 years of e-commerce data. The current e-commerce share of 16.4% represents not a regression to the pre-2020 trend line but a sustained step-change that has held for multiple years post-pandemic.

5. Category Deep Dive: Books vs. Food vs. Nonstore

Books/Hobby/Music has grown at a 25-year CAGR of just 1.7%, while Nonstore Retailers compounded at 9.4% over the same period — illustrating how digitally vulnerable categories stagnated in physical format while online equivalents captured all incremental demand. Food & Beverage's steadier 3.5% 25-year CAGR reflects the friction of perishability, which has buffered grocery from the same indexed collapse.

Deeper Analysis

The first five analyses answered what happened. These four go further: who's accelerating fastest, how COVID hit each category differently, when disruption inflected, and what the most disrupted category's 25-year collapse actually looks like.

6. Growth Acceleration: Who's Speeding Up?

Six of seven categories plot above the diagonal, meaning their 5-year CAGR exceeds their 25-year CAGR — the entire retail landscape is accelerating simultaneously. The two standout outliers are Clothing (+4.3 pp delta) and Books/Hobby/Music (+3.8 pp delta), both of which spent two decades growing slowly and are now posting 5-year CAGRs of 7.2% and 5.6% respectively, driven by belated digital adoption.

7. COVID Impact: Who Collapsed, Who Came Back Stronger?

Clothing suffered the deepest COVID trough at -57.8% versus its 2019 pre-COVID average, yet now sits +25.5% above that same baseline — a full recovery and overshoot driven by e-commerce and athleisure demand shifts. Online Retail stands alone with a positive trough of +7.4% during COVID and a current reading +117.5% above pre-COVID levels, confirming it was the direct beneficiary of every other category's disruption.

8. When Did Disruption Hit? A Rolling View

Electronics' rolling 5-year CAGR has trended near zero for most of the 25-year window, reflecting persistent structural disruption that arrived earlier and more completely than in any other category. Clothing's rolling CAGR has surged in the most recent 5-year window to 7.2%, signaling a disruption wave that peaked later than Electronics but is now hitting with comparable force.

9. The Electronics Collapse: A 25-Year Divergence

Electronics' 25-year CAGR of 0.1% set against the Nonstore Retailers' 9.4% CAGR over the same period is the starkest illustration of e-commerce's zero-sum impact on a single category: online captured essentially all electronics growth while physical retail flatlined. The disruption index score of 100.0 for Electronics is not a relative ranking artifact — it reflects a genuine, near-total transfer of category growth from physical to digital channels over a quarter century.

Key Takeaways

  1. E-commerce has grown 20x from 0.8% to 16.4% of US retail over 25 years, with COVID alone adding a permanent 4.1 percentage points above the pre-pandemic trend.
  2. Electronics is the most disrupted retail category on record with a disruption index of 100.0 and a 25-year CAGR of just 0.1%, meaning online competitors absorbed virtually all category growth for a quarter century.
  3. Clothing's 5-year CAGR of 7.2% is 4.3 percentage points above its 25-year CAGR of 2.9%, marking it as the category currently experiencing the steepest late-stage digital acceleration.
  4. Nonstore retailers are compounding faster now than ever, posting a 5-year CAGR of 13.4% against a 25-year CAGR of 9.4% — e-commerce growth is accelerating, not maturing.
  5. Online Retail's COVID trough was +7.4% above pre-COVID levels while every physical category fell sharply, and it now stands +117.5% above its 2019 baseline, confirming it was the direct demand absorber during the disruption.
  6. Gasoline Stations (disruption index 0.9) and Food & Beverage (disruption index 9.2) remain the most insulated categories, but their 5-year CAGRs of 5.0% and 3.7% show even friction-anchored categories are accelerating under broader retail tailwinds.

Methodology

Data Sources

  • FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data): 9 time series covering total retail, e-commerce, and 6 retail categories
  • Coverage: Monthly data from January 1992; quarterly e-commerce data from Q4 1999
  • All values are seasonally adjusted, in millions of dollars

Key Calculations

  • E-Commerce Share: ECOMSA / (quarterly total retail) * 100
  • CAGR: (end_value / start_value)^(1/years) - 1
  • Disruption Index: (total_retail_CAGR - category_CAGR) / max_gap * 100. Measures how far each category's growth fell below total retail, normalized 0 to 100. Categories growing at the retail average score near zero; the most underperforming category scores 100
  • COVID Acceleration: Linear trend fit on 2000–2019 data, projected forward; excess = actual minus projected
  • COVID Category Impact: Pre-COVID baseline (2019 average) vs. 2020 trough and current quarter, expressed as % change
  • Rolling CAGR: Trailing 5-year compound growth rate recalculated each quarter, showing when disruption inflected for each category

Tools

  • Analysis: Python, pandas, DuckDB, NumPy
  • Visualization: Plotly
  • AI Integration: Claude API (hypothesis generation, insight narration)
  • Report: Jinja2 HTML template

Limitations

  • *Online Retail uses FRED series RSNSR ("Nonstore Retailers"), which includes catalog and mail-order sales alongside e-commerce. By 2025 the non-digital portion is negligible, making it an effective online retail proxy
  • All category series measure sales at physical stores of that type, not total consumer spending in that category. A laptop bought on Amazon counts as Online Retail, not Electronics. This is what makes the Electronics flatline so striking: demand grew, but stores didn't capture it
  • Disruption index is a relative measure benchmarked against total retail growth; a high index means the category grew slower than retail overall, not necessarily that it declined in absolute terms
  • All values are nominal (not inflation-adjusted). Categories with flat nominal growth like electronics have likely declined in real terms
  • Rolling CAGR is sensitive to the starting quarter; 5-year windows smooth most noise but can still reflect idiosyncratic quarter-to-quarter variation

How I Built This

This analysis started with a question I'd been thinking about since working in retail analytics: we all know e-commerce is growing, but which categories actually got hit, and did COVID permanently change the trajectory or was it a blip?

I pulled nine time series from FRED covering 25 years of US retail data and loaded them into DuckDB for fast SQL-based exploration. The analysis pipeline is pure Python: pandas for data manipulation, Plotly for interactive charts, and Jinja2 for the report you're reading now.

Where Claude added real value was at two critical junctures. First, before any analysis ran, I asked Claude to generate testable hypotheses from just the data descriptions. This forced structured thinking about what to look for, and several hypotheses (like Electronics being more disrupted than Books) were genuinely surprising when confirmed. Second, after all charts and metrics were computed, Claude narrated the findings as an executive summary and chart captions, ensuring every insight cites a specific number from the actual data.

The initial analyses answered the core question, but reviewing the results pushed the work further. An early version of the disruption index benchmarked every category against online retailers' 9.4% CAGR, which made gasoline stations (structurally immune to e-commerce) look disrupted. That's not disruption; that's just "grew slower than the fastest channel in retail." Switching to total retail growth as the neutral baseline fixed it: gasoline scores near zero, food barely registers, and electronics stands alone at 100.

Going deeper surfaced stories the initial pass missed. The acceleration scatter revealed that every single category is accelerating post-COVID, but clothing's +4.3pp delta makes it the fastest-moving disruption story right now, not electronics, which most people would guess. The rolling CAGR timeline shows when each category's disruption inflected, which a single 25-year number hides entirely. And the electronics collapse chart (indexed at 103 after 25 years vs. online retail at 1,006) tells the story more viscerally than any bar chart could.
