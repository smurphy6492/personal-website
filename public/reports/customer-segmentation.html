1. Customer Segments

Customers were grouped into 5 segments using K-Means clustering on four features: Recency, Frequency, Monetary, and Tenure (days since first purchase). Adding Tenure matters because without it, clustering can't tell the difference between a new customer with one purchase and a lapsed customer with one purchase. Both have low frequency and low spend, but they're completely different business problems. Champions alone account for 75% of all revenue.

2. Finding the Right Number of Segments

Silhouette analysis favors fewer clusters (K=2 scores 0.362, K=3 scores 0.437), but fewer groups collapse lifecycle stages into one bucket. New customers and lapsed customers look identical at K=3. We chose K=5 (silhouette=0.339) because customer lifecycle needs enough resolution to distinguish Champions, new Potential Loyalists, and Lost customers. The silhouette tradeoff is worth it for segments a marketing team can act on.

The best clustering result isn't the one with the highest score. It's the one your marketing team can build campaigns around.

3. RFMT Feature Analysis

Recency, Frequency, Monetary, and Tenure distributions before clustering. Frequency and Monetary are heavily right-skewed (a few high-value customers dominate), so log-transformation and standardization are applied before clustering to prevent those outliers from distorting the segments.

4. Defining Churn: Data-Driven Threshold

Before predicting churn, we need to define it. Instead of picking an arbitrary inactivity threshold, we used the transaction data to find the point where customers stop coming back.

Inter-Purchase Interval Distribution

Across all repeat customers, 90% of consecutive purchases happen within 134 days. Beyond that window, the customer is outside normal buying behavior.

Retention Curve

For every level of inactivity, we measured what fraction of customers ever came back. The probability of return declines steadily, from ~80% at 10 days to below 50% at ~155 days. At 180 days, fewer than half of inactive customers will return, and the curve continues flattening. Combined with the P90 inter-purchase interval of 134 days, 180 days is a defensible threshold: long enough to avoid false positives from normal purchase gaps, short enough to act on before customers are truly gone.

Defining churn isn't about picking a number. It's about methodology. The inter-purchase distribution tells us what's normal; the retention curve tells us when "inactive" becomes "gone." The threshold should be defensible, not arbitrary.

5. Churn Prediction

With the 180-day threshold established from the retention curve, we defined churn using a temporal split: transactions before 2011-06-12 were used to build features, and customers who made no purchase in the 180 days after that cutoff were labeled as churned.

Churn rates map directly to the lifecycle segments: Lost churn at 84%, while Champions churn at a fraction of that. Segment membership is a useful predictor of churn on its own.

Model Comparison

Two models were trained: Logistic Regression (interpretable baseline) and Random Forest (ensemble). Both used balanced class weights to handle the 48% churn rate.

The ROC curve (left) shows how well each model separates churners from non-churners across all decision thresholds. The further the curve bows toward the top-left, the better. The dashed line is random guessing. Both models are nearly identical (AUC 0.802 vs 0.801) and well above the baseline, confirming that the features predict churn.

The Precision-Recall curve (right) shows the tradeoff when deploying this. As you try to catch more churners (higher recall), precision drops and you start flagging active customers as churners. Both models hold ~80%+ precision up to about 50% recall, meaning you could catch half of all churners while being right 4 out of 5 times.

Both models perform nearly identically. The simpler logistic regression captures the signal just as well as the more complex random forest. Ship the interpretable model. You get the same accuracy plus you can explain every prediction to the business.

What Drives Churn?

Feature importance from the Logistic Regression shows which customer behaviors most strongly predict churn. These are the levers a retention team would focus on.

The feature importance chart (left) ranks which inputs the model relies on most. Recency dominates: how recently a customer purchased is the strongest churn signal. A customer who bought yesterday is far less likely to churn than one who last bought 6 months ago.

The confusion matrix (right) shows the model's predictions vs. reality on the test set. The diagonal cells are correct predictions; the off-diagonal cells are errors. This tells you concretely how many churners the model catches and how many active customers it incorrectly flags.

What Predicts Churn Beyond Recency?

Recency dominates feature importance, but it has an inherent correlation with the churn definition: churn means "no purchase in 180 days," and recency measures "days since last purchase." A customer with high recency is mechanically closer to the churn threshold. The relationship isn't circular (a recent buyer can still churn), but it's worth asking: what else predicts churn after you remove recency?

The feature importance without recency (left) shows what the model relies on when it can't use the most obvious signal. The ROC comparison (right) shows the cost of removing it: AUC drops from 0.802 to 0.769, a decrease of 0.033. The model still predicts well above random, confirming that the other features carry real signal on their own.

Running the model both ways is the honest thing to do. Recency is a legitimate predictor and belongs in a production model, but knowing what else matters gives you a fuller picture of churn drivers.

6. Methodology Notes

Data

UCI Online Retail II dataset: 805,549 transactions from 2009-12-01 to 2011-12-09. ~23% of rows were dropped due to missing Customer ID (anonymous purchases). Cancelled orders and zero-price items were removed.

Temporal Split Design

The churn prediction uses a strict temporal split to prevent data leakage. The 180-day churn window was derived from the retention curve analysis (Section 4), not chosen arbitrarily. Features are computed from transactions before the cutoff (2011-06-12); the churn label comes from behavior in the 180 days after. This mirrors how the model would run in production: predicting future behavior from historical data, with no future information leaking into training features.
